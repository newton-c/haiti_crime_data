The Human Costs of the Crisis in Haiti

The most common way we measure crime is homicides. Killing is often caused by competition between criminal groups; it is used to coerce and intimidate civilians, and is easier to measure than crimes such as kidnapping or trafficking.

Since the assassination of President Jovenel Moïse in July 2021, homicides have spiked as criminal groups fill the void left by a shattered government.

But homicides only tell part of the suffering caused by Haiti's criminal take over. Kidnappings have surged, according to data by the UNODC.

While most countries in the region saw fewer people internally displaced last year, Haiti had a massive increase.

The 430% increase in internally displanced people due to conflict in Haiti from 2021 to 2022 is the largest increase the region has witnessed since data started being collected in 2018. With 106,000 people displace in 2022, Haiti saw more displacements that larger countries with potent criminal groups, such as Brazil and Mexico, have experienced in recent years.

The situation in Haiti has exacerbated hunger initially caused by poverty and natural disaster. An Integrated Food Security Phase Classification analysis estimates that 1,807,955 Haitians are experiencing emergency levels of hunger. This situation is most dire in areas like Cité Solei, which has become a battleground for gangs.

And despite going three years without a case of cholera, cases began surging again last year. The World Health Organization estimates that there has since been almost 60,000 cases of the disease which is nearly absent from the rest of Latin America and the Caribbean.
